title = "Why I resist AI"
date = "2026-05-26"

When Copilot released autocomplete, I had a weird feeling develop inside me. It was helping me do
my job faster, but I resisted it. Then Agents inside VScode, followed by Cursor and Claude. Every
single time AI assisted coding got better, I wish it hadn't. It was taking something away from me,
and I didn't know what.

I used to love writing code by hand, and now I don't. There seems to be no point in writing code
without the AI tools - they are a much faster way to get from idea to implementation quick.
There was a point in time where they were a good autocomplete, but now they can write whole modules,
and fix complex bugs. AI can go through documentation and figure out the best way to fit
a certain library into your app's paradigm. It can go from nothing to a working app in minutes.

#### What did I value?
The thing that attracted me to programming was solving tricky problems myself - I valued being able
to describe the problem in code cleanly and figuring out bugs that others couldn't _all by myself_.
I took pride in the fact that I knew certain interesting details about my programming language that
my friends didn't. There was a piece of me in the program - it represented my ideas and beliefs on
computation, paradigms, languages and so on. And now there isn't, it is a lifeless mechanical entity.

I still am a better programmer than AI (albeit not faster most times), and a better software engineer
on the whole, but I'm quite sure AI will overtake me soon. Even right now, it is able to take architectural 
decisions and implement them without much tweaking on my end. There are times when I have to step
in and do stuff myself, but that number is going down by the week.

This is why I don't like AI. There was a world with something I valued in it, and now that world has
turned on its head. I used to think that I cared about the skill of programming for its own sake. It
is only now that I realize I was lying to myself. I also cared about the fact that not everyone could
do it. A lot of the skills I've developed over the years are no longer exclusive to me, and to be
honest it is a sad feeling.

#### Copium
I have to cope somehow. One way is to go raise chickens on a farm. The other way is to change what
I value. I have recently started taking a liking to a more product-focused way of thinking through
problems. Instead of tunnel-visioning into only the code, one can understand users, how they interface
with your software and anticipate changes in the space you're building for. I have learnt to
appreciate the architecture of software a lot more - foundational models are just one piece of the 
puzzle in trying to solve a complex, real-world problem.

I also understand now that there are aspects one has which sit underneath the abstraction of "skills"
as I describe. Things like the ability to think from first principles. These are truly valuable -
they give one the ability to mould and adapt with the changing times, and always find interesting
stuff to work on.


There will probably come a time to raise those chickens, but it is not now.
